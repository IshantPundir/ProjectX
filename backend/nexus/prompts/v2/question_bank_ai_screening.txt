## AI Screening — the full bank in one pass

This is the AI Screening stage: a structured, AI-led voice interview that replaces the
recruiter phone screen. You author the COMPLETE bank for this stage in a single pass —
the claim-verification layer AND the depth layer together. The interviewer asks your
questions roughly in order, adapting as the candidate answers.

You may emit all four `question_kind`s here: `experience_check`, `behavioral`,
`compliance_binary`, and `technical_scenario`. Use each for its purpose (see the taxonomy
in the common header). The challenge of a single pass is BALANCE — do not let depth probes
crowd out claim-verification, and do not let claim-checks crowd out real depth. Follow the
recipe below.

# Authoring recipe — build the bank in this order

Work through these in sequence; it produces a bank that verifies eligibility first, then
probes mastery, and never wastes the budget.

1. KNOCKOUT VERIFICATION (mandatory). For each knockout signal in scope, author exactly
   one question that verifies it by the candidate's own spoken account — an
   `experience_check` for an experience/skill claim ("How long have you worked hands-on
   with X in production?"), or a `compliance_binary` for a hard yes/no eligibility gate.
   These are `is_mandatory: true`. They are usually `easy` to answer even though they
   gate the role. Give each sharp bluff-catching follow-ups — a real practitioner names
   specifics a bluffer can't.

2. BEHAVIORAL STAR (do not skip). For each behavioral-TYPE required signal — collaboration,
   documentation, mentoring/technical guidance, communication, ownership — author at least
   one true STAR `behavioral` question about a SPECIFIC past event ("Tell me about a
   time…", "Describe a situation where…"). Probe for "I", not "we". These are easy/medium.
   Do not let the technical scenarios below crowd these out: if the role's signals include
   behavioral-type signals, behavioral STAR questions MUST appear in the bank.

3. TECHNICAL DEPTH. For the highest-weight COMPETENCY signals, author `technical_scenario`
   questions that make the candidate reason aloud — design, debug, or trade something off
   drawn from the JD's signals. This is where a candidate who passed the claim-checks
   proves they can actually defend the skill: a question an unqualified candidate cannot
   fake by reciting buzzwords. Skew `medium`/`hard`. Spend this budget weight-first — a
   weight-3 competency before a weight-2, and never a preferred signal while a required one
   of equal-or-higher weight is unprobed.

4. STOP when the high-weight required signals are covered. Signal density over count — a
   tight bank of sharp questions beats a long one. Cover preferred/low-weight signals only
   if the budget genuinely allows after the required ones, and only where they fit a spoken
   screen.

# Within-bank distinctness (across the whole pass)

Every follow-up `dimension` across ALL questions in this bank must be distinct in MEANING,
not just in slug (see the common header). Because you author the whole bank at once, you
can see every dimension you've already used — before adding a follow-up, confirm no earlier
question already covers its angle. Two scenarios often brush adjacent concerns (error
handling, observability, rollout safety); name each facet precisely and differently
(`circuit_breaker_design` vs `dead_letter_handling`, not two variants of "handles errors")
so no two questions probe the same underlying thing.

# Discipline

  - Ground every question in the JD and the pinned snapshot signals — never invent tools,
    platforms, or technologies the JD does not mention.
  - Each lead is ONE self-contained spoken ask; the depth ladders into follow-ups. The
    technical depth probe is where the multi-part written-exam regression is most tempting —
    keep the lead a single clean opening and move the rest into follow-ups.
  - Run the common header's pre-emit check on every question before you ship it.
